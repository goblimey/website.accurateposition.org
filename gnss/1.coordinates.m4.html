Geographical Coordinate Systems

GNSS systems report positions using all sorts of conventions, so we have to look at the different ways that a position can be represented.

This guide from the UK's mapping authority The Ordnance Survey (OS) gives an excellent introduction.

The OS also run this web page which converts a position expressed in one coordinate system into all the others. (Beware: accuracy is limited!)

For use offline, the OS offers this spreadsheet tool which does similar conversions, (maybe more accurately, I haven't checked yet).

What follows is my take on the subject.

The Shape of the Earth

According to current theories the Earth and the Moon were formed about 4.5 billion years ago when two planets collided. The heat generated by the impact caused the two bodies to melt and then merge. Some of the material was ejected and coalesced to form the Moon.

The resulting Earth has a solid core at the centre, mainly iron, very hot but under huge pressure, which stops it from melting. Beyond that is more iron, but liquid. Te rotation of which generates the Earth's magnetic field. Further out from the centre is the mantle, essentially a hot gooey mess of rock, flowing like a very viscous liquid. The solid surface crust floats on top of the mantle.

The crust is broken into large plates which move around slowly, pushed and pulled by convection currents in the mantle.

The material in one of the original two planets was a little denser than the material in the other and there are still huge lumps of this denser material slowly moving around, deep below the surface. On the surface, the tides move huge volumes of water backwards and forwards during the day. Even a nearby mountain can have enough material in it to deflect local gravity slightly. These and other factors cause local gravity to be uneven so that it doesn't always pull directly towards the centre of mass of the Earth.

The result of all this is this is that nothing on the Earth is fixed. There's no solid ground. There's a thin shell, relatively solid, around a huge boiling maelstrom of molten fluid. Convection currents deep inside stir up the material, causing the continents on the surface to move around and change shape very slowly.

The Rate of Continental Drift

The Earth's crust is composed of tectonic plates which move around the surface slowly due to continental drift . The part of the European plate that I live on moves against longitude and latitude at about 2.5 cm per year heading approximately north-east. Meanwhile the USA moves in a different direction and the two plates are moving apart at about 3cm per year. Iceland sits at the point where the two plates part which causes all sorts of geophysical effects, some quite violent.

This slow movement didn't matter so much before accurate GNSS equipment came along, but now it can be detected using equipment costing only a few hundred dollars.

The Ellipsoid

The Earth is held together by its own gravity and is almost, but not quite, a sphere. It spins on an axis, which stretches it very slightly at the Equator and shrinks it very slightly at the poles, making it approximately an ellipsoid.

Geographers have defined a perfect ellipsoid that's close on average to the Earth's real shape. It's called the Ellipsoid (capital E).

The Ellipsoid is not always a convenient tool for making maps, and some cartographers define other ellipsoids that better approximate the shape of the locality that they are interested in.

Because a geographical ellipsoid is very close to a sphere, up until the 1960s at least, the UK Ordnance Survey referred to it as a Spheroid. In 1830, the British Astronomer Royal George Airy defined the Airy Spheroid based on a Prime Meridian set by astronomy carried out at the Greenwich Observatory. In 1936 the OS defined the OSGB36 coordinate system, based on Airy's spheroid. They still use OSGB36 for their paper maps. More on that Here

The Geoid

As already mentioned, gravity doesn't always point at the centre of mass of the Earth. There's another solid shape called The Geoid (which just means "Earth-shaped" in Latin).

Where the land meets the sea. the Geoid is approximately at mean sea level, so it's a more formal version of that concept.

According to Wikipedia: "The Geoid is the shape that the ocean surface would take under the influence of the gravity of Earth, including gravitational attraction and Earth's rotation, if other influences such as winds and tides were absent. This surface is extended through the continents."

You can find more about The Geoid here .

As with ellipsoids, mapmakers often define their own geoids which more closely match their local conditions.

To sum up, the Ellipsoid is a regular solid that approximates to the shape of the Earth and the Geoid is an irregular solid that also approximates to the shape of the Earth. The two are designed to work globally but local ellipsoids and geoids can approximate the local landscape more conveniently for local maps.

Latitude and Longitude

Geographers divide up the Ellipsoid using the familiar imaginary lines of longitude and latitude. A position on the surface of the Ellipsoid can be specified in degrees, minutes and seconds of longitude and latitude. A minute is one sixtieth of a degree of arc and a second is one sixtieth of a minute.

The lines of latitude (or "parallels") are circles running east-west around the Ellipsoid. The Equator is the line of latitude equidistant from the north and south poles. That's zero latitude. The circles of latitude are different sizes. The Equator is the biggest and the circles of latitude get smaller the closer you get to one of the poles.

The circles of latitude are labelled by degrees running from -90 degrees at the south pole through zero at the Equator and +90 degrees at the north pole. The city of Newcastle upon Newcastle-upon-Tyne in the UK is roughly on the line of latitude at 55 degrees. So your latitude tells you how far north or south you are from the Equator.

The lines of longitude (or "meridians") are lines running north-south from the north pole to the south pole around the Ellipsoid. Because the earth is ellipsoidal rather than a sphere, these are ellipses, not circles. They are all the same length. The Prime Meridian runs through the grounds of Greenwich Observatory in London. It has a longitude of zero degrees. Your longitude tells you how far east or west you are from the Prime Meridian.

The poles, the equator and the positions of the lines of latitude, are real objects, arising from the Earth's rotation. In contrast, the position of the prime meridian is entirely arbitrary. It's wherever people agree that it is. The Greenwich Observatory was set up by the British Admiralty, who ran what was at the time the world's most powerful navy. They decided where the prime meridian would lie and everybody else accepted it.

The French government had their own favourite meridian, which ran through the Paris Observatory. In its first version, the metre was defined as one ten millionth of the distance from the Equator to the North Pole along that meridian. Actually, that distance changes as the Earth changes shape, so the definition has been refined using more stable metrics.

If France had won the Napoleonic Wars, we would probably have all accepted that meridian as the line of zero longitude.

In fact, the Greenwich Observatory has moved the prime meridian around its grounds several times over the years and in any case it moves all the time due to continental drift.

That didn't used to matter because most surveying equipment wasn't accurate enough to notice, but now that we have GNSS receivers accurate to two or three centimetres, it does matter. In fact, it's become convenient to define several versions of latitude and longitude, each with meridians in different places.

This page from the UK Open University explains how to use some very simple equipment to find your longitude and latitude relative to the Prime Meridian and the Equator. The more accurate your measuring equipment and clock, the more accurate will be your result.

Longitude, Latitude and Distance

If my GNSS equipment gives me the longitude and latitude of two position, how far apart are they along the curved surface of the Ellipsoid?

One important point to understand about all measuring equipment is that just because a gadget displays its result to lots of decimal places, doesn't mean that it is that accurate.

My Trimble Catalyst GNSS receiver is accurate to two or three centimetres. It displays its position in degrees, minutes and seconds, and the seconds value is displayed to eight decimal places, That's far better resolution than the equipment can actually manage. We'll see that you can ignore all but the first few decimal places of the seconds part of the coordinates.

Distance Along a Line of Longitude

For rough purposes we can pretend that the Ellipsoid is a sphere, which it almost is, so all the meridians are circles of the same length. The earth has radius 6,371,000 metres, diameter 12,742,000 metres. The circumference of a circle is

diameter X pi

so the circumference of a meridian is about 40,000,000 metres.

On a meridian, a difference of latitude of one degree covers about 111,111 metres That's worked out as follows:

Note that there are 360 degree or 1,296,000 seconds in a circle. These circles are 40,000,000 metres around, so:

  • one degree covers about 111,111 metres
  • one minute covers about 1,851 metres
  • one second covers about 31 metres
  • 0.001 seconds covers about three centimetres
  • 0.0001 seconds covers about three millimetres

In a test with my Trimble Catalyst device fixed in one place, the degrees and minutes values for the latitude remained steady, as you would expect, given the resolution. The seconds value varied from 11.6873 to 11.6875. That represents a range of about six millimetres in my position north to south.

Distance Along a Line of Latitude

The lines of latitude are circles of different sizes so the calculation a little bit more complicated.

I live in the UK, which lies between the 50 degree parallel and the 60 degree parallel. Our nation is small enough that for a rough estimate we can work out the length of one parallel and use that result everywhere. 55 degrees latitude is in the middle of the UK. That's about the position of the fair city of Newcastle-upon-Tyne. (According to Wikipedia, the centre of Newcastle is at 54.9783 degrees latitude).

To work out the length of the 55 degree parallel, use this picture:

We're interested in the length r, which is the radius of the 55 degree parallel.

The hypotenuse of the right-angled triangle in the picture is a radius of the circle. Newcastle is at the end of that line. The Earth has radius 6,371,000 metres so that's the length of that line.

The sine of an angle is:

(length of opposite side) divided by (length of hypotenuse),

so given the angle 35 degrees we can work out r. I get 3,654,255 metres.

The circumference of a circle of radius r is

2 X pi X r

A spreadsheet will work the whole thing out for you. Microsoft Excel's sine function works in radians, not degrees. To convert the angle to radians, multiply by pi/180.

If my mathematical working is correct, you can use this Excel formula to calculate the circumference of the 55 degree parallel:

=(SIN(35*PI()/180)*6371000*2*PI())

which gives me about 22,960,364 metres for the UK, roughly half the circumference of one of the meridians.

Dividing that by 360 X 60 X 60 gives the distance covered by one second of in the UK - about 18 metres. So 0.001 seconds of latitude here covers about 2 centimetres east to west, and 0.0001 seconds covers about two millimetres.

If my GNSS receiver is accurate to two or three centimetres and I'm working in the UK, I should believe the first two decimal places of the seconds value of the longitude, be a bit suspicious of the third decimal place.

In my test, the value for longitude varied from 9.7803 seconds to 9.7808 seconds. In terms of distance those values range over about a centimetre.

As well as latitude and longitude, the Catalyst displays its orthonometric height (the height above the Geoid). Apparently this will figure will usually be less accurate than the other two, maybe 5 centimetres rather than two or three. In my test, the height value varied from 201.77 metres to 201.78 metres, so it ranged over one centimetre.

Vertical and Horizontal

As we will see, some concepts that we think are very simple and obvious turn out out to be a little more complicated. The concept of something being "vertically above" something else, is an example. In the Eighteenth Century, Nevil Maskelyne, the UK's Astronomer Royal, showed that a plumb line close to an isolated mountain will deviate slightly from the vertical due to the gravitational pull of the nearby huge mass of rock.

This was a nuisance to astronomers because they used plumb lines and baths of mercury to align their telescopes, assuming that the plumb line would be vertical and the surface of the mercury would be horizontal. They could work out the position of their telescope by taking sighting of celestial objects, but a deviation in local gravity of just a few seconds would put the result out by metres.

To make matters worse, we now know that you don't need a nearby mountain to interfere with your. The tides and those big lumps of denser matter under the surface also mess things up.

Sure enough, this is exactly what happened in 1830 when George Airy's team at the Greenwich Observatory measured the position of the prime meridian and put a mark on the ground showing where they thought it was. Greenwich is a long way from the nearest mountain, but the mark was about 100 metres from the correct position. Read about that here .

Since the line was fixed the ground beneath it has moved again by about a few more metres due to continental drift.

If your plumb line doesn't show you the vertical, then what does? This was one reason for the invention of the Geoid.

Page 16 of the OS Guide discusses this issue. That guide also explains why national mapmakers define other ellipsoids that approximate better to their local landscape.

The Ordnance Survey ran a complete re-survey of the UK from 1935 to 1962. At the end they produced a 400 page report that you can download here . Among other things, it describes how they used telescopes at the Herstmonceaux Observatory to get a more accurate position of the Prime Meridian.

The Poles

The Earth's axis of spin runs through the centre of mass. We call the points where this axis emgerges from the Ellipsoid the North and South Poles. The position of those objects was measured with increasing accuracy from the early twentieth century onwards. In 1984 the Bureau International d'Heure produced what became the International Terrestrial Reference Frame , which defines everything needed to specify a position on the Earth - the Poles, the Ellipsoid, the Equator etc. Because of continental drift this is a moving target and the International Earth Rotation Service (IERS) updates the definition every few years to keep it in line with reality.

Height

Your angle of longitude, angle of latitude and height identify your position anywhere on the Earth. But height above what?

Your geoidal height or orthometric height is your height above or below the Geoid.

Your Ellipsoidal Height is your height above or below the Ellipsoid.

You can also represent your height as your height above local mean sea level. That will be close to, but not always the same as your geoidal height.

In the 1920's the UK Ordnance Survey established a measuring station at Newlyn in Cornwall. Essentially it's vertical tube, partly immersed in the sea, with a float sitting on the surface of the water. The float goes up and down with the tide. Engineers took regular readings of the position of the float to establish mean sea level. UK maps show the height of a position as an offset from this datum.

Here in the UK there's a phenomenon called The Post-Glacial Rebound . During the last ice age, ice a few kilometres thick covered Scotland, Wales and most of England, getting thinner, and therefore lighter, as it approached the south coast, stopping altogether just short of it.

The weight of the ice pushed Scotland downwards and in response the southern end of England lifted. Since the ice melted the land has been bouncing back, which causes Scotland to rise from the sea by a couple of millimetres each year and the south of England to sink in response. This increases the effect of sea level rise in the south of England and decreases it in the North of Scotland.

Further north the ice was even thicker and the land pushed down even further. Parts of northern Europe are rising at one centimetre per year.

Meanwhile, sea levels are rising across the world due to global warming. The effect is bigger in the UK than the post-glacial rebound, so the overall effect is that sea levels are rising everywhere in the UK, but they are rising a bit faster in the south than in the north.

So UK Mean Sea Level was defined a century ago and sea levels have risen steadily since then. Strictly, this doesn't matter, because UK mean sea level was always just an arbitrary value - the mean level of the sea around the coast of the UK in places away from Newlyn can be different due to local currents and other factors.

Page 16 of the OS Guide discusses this issue.

Different GNSS devices display your height using one of ellipsoidal height, geoidal height or height above mean sea level. Geoidal height and height above mean sea level are usually similar but not the same. Ellipsoidal height can be very different from the other two measures at the same position.

What time is it?

Our modern notion of time and time zones is based on the Prime Meridian. In days gone by, noon was the time of day when the Sun was at its highest in the sky, which happened at different times even just across the UK. When the railways came, we needed a common notion of the time of day so that the operators could publish a timetable. That lead to Railway Time, which was based on Noon at the Greenwich Observatory. Railway Time was later replaced by Greenwich Mean Time (GMT) and then Coordinated Universal Time (UTC).

As explained here, the sun is at its highest point above the Prime Meridian at different times of day over the year, varying over about 16 minutes. Noon UTC is set to the mean of those times, so everybody's notion of the time of day depends upon the precise position of the Prime Meridian, which is now based on the work of IERS.

People follow different timezones around the world but they are all based on UTC. In winter in the UK we use the GMT timezone, which follows UTC - midnight GMT is midnight UTC. In the summer, we use British Summer Time (BST), which is one hour ahead of UTC. Other countries do something similar but call it one timezone - in France they use Central European Time (CET) which is one hour ahead of UTC in the winter (midnight CET is 1am UTC) and two hours ahead in the summer.

Terrestrial Reference Frames

A network of observatories ("ground stations") around the world constantly measure the distance to each GNSS satellite very accurately. The results drive the calculations for Precise Point Positioning, which is used to correct the results of a GNSS survey.

A side effect is that the positions of the ground stations are known very accurately. These positions are published, along with the velocity of the station (in other words, the velocity of the ground beneath it) in metres per year.

Since everything on the Earth's surface is moving and a position only makes sense relative to another position, what does it mean to "fix" the Equator and Prime Meridian?

One way is to fix them at the mean of the movement of all of the continental plates. That's the basis of the International Terrestrial Reference Frame (ITRF).

If you measure the longitude and latitude of a "fixed" object on the ground today within this reference frame, then come back and do it again a few years later, you will get a different answer. That line on the ground at the Greenwich Observatory moves north-east relative to the ITRF at about 2.5 centimetres per year.

For the purposes of land surveying, this is very inconvenient because any position measurements that you take gradually become out of date. If a utility company buries a pipe in the ground and wants to come back twenty years later to do some maintenance, finding where to dig quickly and accurately saves money. Having to correct the position measurement for the passage of time is a nuisance.

Another way to represent your position is to tie an entirely notional Equator and Prime Meridian to fixed points on the ground on your local continental plate. Within that reference frame your continental plate and everything on it stays still and all the other plates move. Your latitude, longitude and height stay the same.

This is essentially what the UK Ordnance Survey did when they defined OSGB36. Their points on the ground were concrete markers called Trig Points that they laid out across the country. Nowadays, the GNSS ground stations act as the fixed points that define the various reference frames.

The European Terrestrial Reference System and the associated European Terrestrial Reference Frame provide a coordinate system which is fixed to points on the ground in Europe. There are similar reference frames for other regions.

This convention works locally but after a few years your notion of the Poles and the Equator drift away from measurable reality. The poles of your reference frame no longer match the Earth's axis of spin.

To stop the various reference frames getting too far out of kilter with each other and with reality, they are reset every few years to pull them back to the International Terrestrial Reference Frame.

When you record a position using a GNSS receiver, you must also record which reference frame you used and which version of it.

The same point applies to the GNSS satellites. The GNSS system depends on published predictions of their positions in space within some Earth-based reference frame. To make sense of the positions, you have to know which reference frame is being used and, if necessary, convert the positions to the frame that you are using.

Universal Transverse Mercator Coordinates

In the early twentieth century, The UK Ordnance Survey used longitude and latitude to define positions as seen on this 1938 map of Guildford . If you look at the corners of the map and zoom in, you'll see that it shows longitude along the top and bottom and latitude along each side.

In the 1940s the OS and some other national mapping authorities adopted Universal Transverse Mercator (UTM) coordinates. This approach divides the Ellipsoid into 60 zones, each covering about six degrees of longitude. The local surface of each zone is projected onto a flat map and each position is represented by its distance along an east-west line (its easting) from a zero point and its distance along a north-south line (its northing) from the same point. More details on UTM are here.

A position in three dimensions can be represented by its easting, its northing and its height above some base such as mean sea level. However, making a flat map representing objects on a curved surface involves compromises, even in a small country like the UK.

During its 1925 to 1962 re-survey of the UK, The Ordnance Survey created the National Grid reference system (OSGB, strictly OSGB36), also known as British National Grid (BNG). More about that here .

The zero point for OSGB eastings and northings is out at sea to the south-west of Land's End. The notional Mean Sea Level was fixed in the 1920's using the facility at Newlyn.

OS maps printed since the re-survey have shown eastings and northings around the edges. Contour lines show height above sea level.

OSGB is defined in terms of reference points on the ground - in the mid twentieth century, a network of physical markers called Trig Points. The most recognisable of these are four-foot high concrete pillars built at strategic points around the country, usually on high ground so that they can be seen from a distance. These trig points move with continental drift so the OSGB36 grid moves with them.

Earth-Centred Earth-Fixed (ECEF or "Cartesian") Coordinates

The GNSS satellites follow an elliptical orbit, one focus of which is the centre of mass of the Earth. It's fairly natural to specify each satellite's position relative to that point. ECEF format uses cartesian (x, y, z) coordinates measured in metres from that centre. So the coordinates are "Earth Centred".

They're "Earth-fixed" because the origin of the X, Y and Z axes is the centre of the earth. and the axes are fixed to lines of longitude and latitude. The X axis runs from the North Pole to the South Pole, the Y axis runs through the centre of the Earth and the intersection of the Prime Meridian and the Equator (longitude 0, latitude zero). The Z axis is perpendicular to the other two. (it emerges at latitude zero, longitude 90.) For more details of ECEF see here .

As we've seen, the position of the poles and the equator depend on which terrestrial reference frame you are using, so the same goes for ECEF coordinates. You need to know which frame the cartesian axes are bound to.

The published positions of the satellites that the receiver uses to find its own position have to make sense everywhere, so they are specified in the WGS84 reference Frame (which is independent of the drifting continental plates).

Given that the positions of the satellites are specified by ECEF coordinates bound to the WGS84 reference frame, the GNSS receiver figures out its position within the same reference frame. For convenience, some receivers converts that result to some other coordinate system, such as latitude and longitude bound to the European Terrestrial Reference Frame and height above some suitable geoid.

Latitude, longitude and height is a convenient form if you're drawing a map, but if you want to work out the distance between two positions, ECEF is better. That's the kind of job that the cartesian coordinate system was invented for. If two positions close enough together that you can ignore the curvature of the Earth, you just find the difference in the positions along each axis and use Pythagoras' Theorem.

Unfortunately, all of my centimetre-accurate GNSS devices only display positions as latitude, longitude and height.

Conversion to UTM is more difficult than to latitude, longitude and height, which is a nuisance here in the UK because for the last sixty years we've been creating records of positions using Ordnance Survey grid references. Their coordinate transformation web page converts a position from a grid reference to all of the other formats and back again, but it introduces a significant error, anything up to a metre.

So I have GNSS equipment that can find my position within a couple of centimetres, but if I use the website to convert it to an OS grid reference, I lose a lot of that accuracy.

Another of my GNSS devices is a Garmin Etrax, a hiking aid. It doesn't have a correction system so its accuracy can be no more than three metres. It displays my position using Ordnance Survey notation, easting, northing and height above sea level. Internally it must be using ECEF format, because that's how GNSS works. It's then converting the result to OSGB36 for display, but I have no idea what algorithm it's using or what that the error in the result is.

What Three Words

The What Three Words system divides the world into 3-metre squares and gives each square a three-word code. For example "pretty.needed.chill" represents a point by the river Cherwell near Oxford in the UK.

The 3-meter resolution matches that of a typical unassisted GNSS receiver.

This is a very convenient way to record positions - the Surrey Portable Antiquities Scheme recommends its use for reporting finds.

Recording a position at that accuracy is better than nothing, but bear in mind that the real position is somewhere within three metres in all directions, so within a six-metre wide circle. If an archaeologist wants to come back and search that area later, they may have to dig a very big hole to find the right spot.

Archaeologists have to dig holes to find things but they are painfully aware that by doing so they are destroying other archaeology that could be found in the future using techniques not yet invented, so they want the hole to be as small as possible. Now that they can afford more accurate equipment, they are starting to use it.
