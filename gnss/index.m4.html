Global Navigation Satellite Systems (GNSS)

GNSS is a generic term for a group ("constellation") of satellites that send out radio signals which help a receiver to find its position. The first and best known constellation was the American Global Position System (GPS) but there are now four constellations of navigation satellites that cover the whole of the Earth - GPS, the Russian Glonass, the Chinese BeiDou and the European Union's Galileo. Some satellites from each of those constellations should be visible from any point on the Earth's surface with a clear view of the sky. There are other constellations that give limited coverage.

GNSS receivers are available that can use the data from any or all of these satellites to find their position.

The use of a GNSS receiver to find a route to some destination is well-known. Satellite Navigation (SatNav) systems have been used by drivers, sailors and pedestrians for many years and nowadays the same technology is built into smartphones. A SatNav contains a GNSS Receiver, a digital map stored using some sort of Graphical Information System (GIS) and a sophisticated piece of software that keeps track of where the device is within the map and figures out a good route from there to wherever its user wants to end up.

Other types of GNSS receiver are used just to find the device's current position. These are used for applications such as land surveying.

A single GNSS device can find its position to within about three metres. That's adequate for a SatNav but land surveying needs more accuracy. As we will see, Survey-quality GNSS devices have been around for twenty years or more. They used to be very expensive and initially only large organisations could afford to use them. The UK's mapping authority the Ordnance Survey was an early adopter. Over the last few years, prices have fallen dramatically and accurate GNSS devices are now available for a few hundred dollars, making it feasible for a local architect, a builder or an individual enthusiast like me to use them.

I'm working on a proposal wherein young people (aged eleven to eighteen years) will use accurate GNSS receivers to measure the effects of continental drift.
